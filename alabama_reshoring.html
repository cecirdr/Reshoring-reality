Alabama's
Reshoring Moment

The national reshoring opportunity isn't abstract for Alabama — it's already landing. From Huntsville's defense and space command boom to Mobile's naval shipbuilding expansion and the first new integrated aluminum mill in America in 40 years, Alabama is at the center of industries the U.S. most urgently needs to rebuild. Here's what's happening, what workforce it demands, and what our universities and community colleges need to scale up.

⭐ MOST ACTIVE SECTOR · North Alabama
Aerospace, Defense, Missiles & Drones
Boeing · Lockheed Martin · Raytheon · Northrop Grumman · BAE Systems · L3Harris · NASA MSFC · ULA · Performance Drone Works
$15B+
Active contracts & investments flowing through Redstone in 2025–2026 alone
The Patriot missile contract ($9.8B, Sept 2025) was the headline — but it's one of more than a dozen major weapons programs now concentrated in Huntsville. The full portfolio running through Redstone right now: Patriot · THAAD · Standard Missile (9 variants) · Tomahawk · Javelin · JASSM · Precision Strike Missile (PrSM) · HELLFIRE/JAGM · Dark Eagle hypersonic weapon · and unmanned systems. Raytheon completed a $115M expansion in March 2026, increasing missile integration capacity by 50%. Lockheed Martin opened a $17.1M Hypersonics System Integration Lab in December 2025. Boeing has invested $200M+ since 2024 in its PAC-3 seeker factory — Secretary of Defense Hegseth visited in December 2025. Under the "Arsenal of Freedom" initiative, Boeing, Raytheon, Lockheed, Northrop, BAE, and L3Harris have all agreed to quadruple production of precision weapons, with Huntsville facilities central to each ramp-up. And Performance Drone Works — a homegrown Huntsville company — opened Drone Factory 01 in August 2025 (500 jobs), just raised $110M in April 2026, and already holds contracts across every US military branch. US Space Command HQ (~1,400 jobs) is moving from Colorado. Golden Dome is coming. Army Space and Missile Defense Command has nearly quadrupled in size. This is not incremental growth — it is a generational defense buildup centered on one Alabama city.
Directly addresses: Defense electronics #1 reshore priority · hypersonics · autonomous systems

⭐ FASTEST GROWING · South Alabama · Mobile
Naval Shipbuilding & Maritime
Austal USA · Alabama Shipyard (acquiring) · Silver Ships · Master Boat Builders
$750M
Two new facilities under construction · 2,000 new jobs · both operational 2026
Austal USA is undergoing its most dramatic transformation since it opened. Two major facilities are under construction simultaneously: MMF3 — a 369,600 sq ft submarine module fabrication facility (Phase 1 summer 2026, full ops late 2026/early 2027) building Columbia- and Virginia-class submarine modules with digital twins and automation; and FA2 — a 192,000 sq ft large steel ship assembly facility building Offshore Patrol Cutters and TAGOS-25 ocean surveillance ships. Together: $750M invested, 2,000 new jobs, workforce growing from 3,100 to 5,000+. Austal VP Larry Ryder said in September 2025 they're "a little behind on hiring" — the buildings open faster than the trained workers are arriving. Alabama Shipyard next door is also being acquired to add Columbia- and Virginia-class module capacity and additive manufacturing. The US Navy has declared "urgent" need for submarine production — Mobile is the answer.
Directly addresses: US naval shipbuilding rebuild · submarine industrial base

Automotive Manufacturing
Honda (Lincoln) · Mercedes (Vance) · Hyundai (Montgomery) · Toyota/Mazda (Huntsville)
$15B+
Total OEM investment in Alabama · 53,000 direct jobs · 1.3M vehicle annual capacity
The most recent data makes Alabama's automotive story even stronger than before. Mercedes announced a $4 billion investment in March 2026 — adding the GLC and a new core-segment model to Tuscaloosa, where its 6,000 workers support an estimated 60,000 supplier jobs. Honda hit 7 million vehicles produced in January 2026; the 2026 Passport and Ridgeline are both built exclusively in Alabama. Toyota began differential production for Tacoma, Tundra, and Sequoia in October 2025 ($282M, 350 new jobs). Hyundai assembled 362,000 vehicles in 2025. Critically: tariffs are actually driving all four OEMs toward more US production, not less — Alabama is a direct beneficiary of that dynamic with existing plants, established workforces, and ready supply chains.

South Alabama · Mobile Aeroplex
Commercial Aviation Manufacturing
Airbus US Manufacturing · Continental Aerospace · 22 relocated Airbus suppliers
2,800
Airbus Mobile employees after Oct 2025 expansion · 3,200 total site workforce
Airbus inaugurated its second A320neo final assembly line in October 2025 — making Mobile one of only three locations in the Western world where A320s are assembled (the others: Hamburg, Germany). The expansion doubled Mobile's production area to over 1 million sq ft and grew the workforce from ~1,800 to 2,800. The 100th Alabama-built A220 was delivered in July 2025. Airbus is also targeting 14 A220s per month globally by 2026. 22 Airbus suppliers have relocated to Mobile County since the plant opened in 2015 — creating a genuine aerospace industrial cluster, not just one factory. A third assembly line is being evaluated for the future.
Connected to: Aerospace supply chain · allied manufacturing investment model

Must
Reshore priority #1

The national analysis identified defense electronics as a non-negotiable reshore. Huntsville already is that reshore — and 2025–2026 has compressed a decade of growth into 18 months. Raytheon's March 2026 expansion added 50%+ missile integration capacity. Lockheed opened a hypersonics lab in December 2025. Boeing invested $200M+ in its PAC-3 seeker factory. Performance Drone Works raised $110M in April 2026 for its homegrown Huntsville drone factory. The Arsenal of Freedom initiative has all six major primes agreeing to quadruple precision weapons output — with Huntsville facilities on each program. The constraint isn't contracts, investment, or facility space. It is engineers, technicians, and cybersecurity professionals — and the universities and community colleges that produce them.

Workforce adaptability
#1
Auto export state
Alabama's auto leadership is powertrain-agnostic — but the workforce needs to be too
Alabama is the #1 auto exporting state in the US, and that position doesn't depend on EVs winning. Honda, Mercedes, Hyundai, and Toyota are all hedging across ICE, hybrid, and EV simultaneously — and US consumer demand for pure EVs has come in well below earlier projections. The real question isn't which powertrain wins. It's whether Alabama workers can build whichever one the market demands. Hybrids dominate the next decade? High-voltage training still matters. EVs accelerate in fleet or commercial segments? Same skills. The $30M AIDT EV Technology Center isn't a bet on EVs winning — it's insurance that Alabama's 40,000 automotive workers can build whatever rolls off the line.
The retention problem
Brain drain
Systemic risk
Alabama trains talent it doesn't keep
Alabama legislators have flagged it directly: the state attracts in-state students but graduates leave. With Huntsville booming, Mobile expanding, and $7B+ in new annual investment, the economic case to stay has never been stronger — but only if students know the jobs exist. The STEM Council, AIDT, and ACCS all need more explicit career pathway marketing connecting programs to the specific employers and salaries waiting for graduates who stay.
Instructor pay crisis
Gap
Structural barrier
Community college instructors earn less than the industry jobs they teach toward
This is the most under-discussed structural problem in Alabama workforce development. An Austal welder earns more than a welding instructor. An ArcelorMittal process technician earns more than the ACCS instructor teaching process technology. You cannot build the workforce pipeline if the teachers keep leaving for industry. Alabama needs instructor pay reform or industry co-funding of adjunct instructor positions for practitioners.
// The Alabama bottom line
Alabama is not waiting for reshoring to arrive — it is already one of the best-positioned states in the country to capture the reshoring moment across defense, advanced metals, automotive, shipbuilding, and aviation. The investments are real: $4B+ in aluminum, $1.6B in electrical steel, $10B in naval shipbuilding backlog, $9.8B in missile defense, Space Command HQ, and the 4th-largest commercial aviation manufacturing hub in the world sitting in Mobile.

The constraint is the same one we identified nationally: workforce. Not investment. Not policy. Not land. Workers. Specifically — shipbuilding tradespeople for Austal's 10,000-job gap, cybersecurity engineers for Redstone's acceleration, EV technicians for the automotive transition, and metals process workers for Novelis and ArcelorMittal's plants that open in 2027.

The educational infrastructure — UAH, Auburn, UA, UAB, Alabama A&M, and 24 ACCS community colleges — is the lever. The programs that need the most urgent attention are the ones that don't yet exist: naval architecture at South Alabama, electrical steel process technology at Coastal Alabama Bay Minette, and scaled cybersecurity capacity everywhere near Huntsville. Every year those programs don't launch is a year Alabama's employers hire from somewhere else — or leave the jobs unfilled.
