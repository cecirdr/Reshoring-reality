Strategic Supply Chain Brief // Allied Network Analysis

The Western Supply Web
Partial Deglobalization Done Right

What the US reshoring challenge actually looks like if we don't try to do it alone — mapping the allied network that already exists, what each partner uniquely provides, where the relationships stand today, and what gaps remain even with full cooperation.

The premise: Full domestic self-sufficiency is environmentally impossible and demographically implausible. But a coordinated western-aligned supply web — North America, key Asian allies, selective EU partnerships, and Latin America — can cover most critical gaps within 10–20 years rather than 30–40. The question is whether the political will to cooperate survives long enough to let it work.

What the western network still cannot cover — even with full allied cooperation
Heavy rare earth processing at scale
Dysprosium and terbium — critical for defense magnets, EV motors, and wind turbines — have no western source at production scale. Australia and Brazil have the deposits. China has 20+ years of processing knowledge lead. Even with maximum investment, heavy REE independence is a 15–20 year project and requires building institutional knowledge from near-zero.
Battery-grade graphite anodes
China processes ~85% of battery-grade graphite globally. Australia has developing graphite projects. There is no credible western alternative at EV-scale within 5 years. This is the most dangerous hidden gap in the battery supply chain — less discussed than lithium but equally critical and harder to replace.
Solar PV manufacturing at scale
China produces ~80% of global solar wafers. No western nation — alone or collectively — has comparable manufacturing scale or cost structure. Germany and Japan have the technology; no one has the gigafactory infrastructure. A coordinated US-EU-Japan solar manufacturing initiative is theoretically possible but would require 15+ years and massive coordinated subsidy to achieve price competitiveness.
Mineral refining & processing capacity
Latin America has the ore. Australia has the ore. The bottleneck is processing and refining — and most of it still flows to China because China built the world's refining infrastructure when no one else invested. Building allied refining capacity in Chile, Texas, and Australia is the single highest-leverage investment the western network can make — but it requires 10–15 years and sustained capital.
US domestic shipbuilding
South Korea can bridge commercial shipbuilding. It cannot fix US Jones Act requirements or rebuild US shipbuilding institutional knowledge. This gap is not solvable through allied cooperation alone — it requires domestic investment in yards, workforce, and the kind of sustained industrial policy the US has not demonstrated capacity for. A 25–40 year project.
Talent for industrial R&D
The allied network solves supply chain and materials gaps. It does not solve the US workforce pipeline problem. No amount of allied cooperation generates the rare earth process engineers, mining engineers, and materials scientists the US needs domestically. That requires K–12 investment, maintained immigration policy, and salary parity with tech — all domestic policy problems no trade deal can fix.
Policy consistency across election cycles
Every partner in this network is watching US reliability. Australia, Japan, Chile, and Argentina have made real commitments based on an assumption of sustained US engagement. If the US reverses course on allied cooperation with the next administration change, these partners will hedge toward China — and the window to rebuild the aligned supply web will close, possibly permanently, as China deepens its processing dominance.
Latin American refining sovereignty
Chile ships raw copper to China for refining. Brazil's REEs are barely extracted. Peru sells to whoever pays. Chinese capital already dominates Latin American mining investment in Peru and Bolivia. The window to re-route value chains toward western refiners is open — but requires the US to offer a credible economic alternative that's more attractive than Chinese capital with fewer conditions attached.
// The honest bottom line — what partial deglobalization through the western network actually achieves
If the US pursues coordinated partial deglobalization through the western-aligned network — rather than unilateral autarky — the outlook changes dramatically. Pharmaceuticals are solvable within a decade. Battery supply chains are solvable within 15 years. Semiconductor independence, with Taiwan as anchor and Korea/Japan as co-investors, is achievable within 20 years. Base metal supply from Latin America + Australia is already largely secured.

What remains genuinely hard even with full allied cooperation: heavy rare earth processing, graphite, solar scale, and shipbuilding. These are 20–40 year projects regardless of approach.

The math is clear: the allied network approach compresses a 30–40 year solo reshoring timeline into 10–20 years for most critical needs, at a fraction of the environmental and economic cost. The obstacles are not technical or geological. They are political — specifically, the US capacity to sustain cooperative, non-combative relationships with its partners across multiple election cycles. That has been the variable all along. It still is.
