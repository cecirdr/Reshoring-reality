🇹🇼 TSMC (AZ fabs building) · 🇰🇷 Samsung/SK Hynix · 🇳🇱 ASML (EUV — irreplaceable)
Manpower Critical
~45K researchers; 40–45% foreign-born. TSMC Arizona already struggling to find US-trained process engineers despite billions invested.
Education gap Severe
EE + materials science PhD pipeline is thin and heavily foreign-born. Tightening immigration simultaneously would cut the workforce in half overnight.
Environmental impact Moderate
Intensive water use (fabs need ultrapure water by the millions of gallons). Chemical waste (etchants, solvents). Arizona water stress is real. Contained but significant.
Why this verdict — and why allied-assisted is the only realistic model
Full domestic semiconductor independence from scratch is a 15+ year project that the US workforce cannot staff without maintained immigration. The allied model (TSMC Arizona + ASML tools + Korean memory + Dutch optics) gets to meaningful capacity by 2029–33 rather than 2038+. ASML's EUV lithography machines cannot be replicated — the Netherlands is a permanent non-negotiable partner in this supply chain. Attempting purely domestic would mean no advanced chips until at least 2040.

~75K researchers; best-maintained pipeline of all 10 sectors. Medicinal chemistry programs are healthy. Bottleneck is manufacturing process chemistry, not drug discovery.

Strong drug discovery pipeline but thin on manufacturing process chemistry — which is a different skill from inventing drugs. Factory-floor pharma expertise is largely offshore.

Chemical solvents, pharmaceutical waste streams, water contamination risk. Manageable with modern facilities but not trivial. Concentrated in manufacturing corridors.

Full reshore would dramatically raise drug prices for ordinary Americans with minimal security benefit. The right model: reshore antibiotics, blood thinners, and defense-critical medications; leave routine generics to India + EU. The EU-US trade deal explicitly provides zero/near-zero tariffs on generic pharma ingredients — a deliberate policy win already in place.

Manpower High
~18K electrochemistry/materials researchers. Pipeline lags industrial demand by 8–12 years. Korea and Japan have the scale knowledge; US is building from near-zero.
Education gap High
Electrochemistry and battery engineering programs are growing but small. Most battery cell manufacturing expertise globally sits in Asia.
Environmental impact High

The graphite gap — the most dangerous hidden problem in this sector
Cell assembly domestically is achievable and already happening (Korea/Japan firms building US plants). Raw lithium is covered by Australia + Latin America. But graphite anodes remain the dangerous blind spot — China processes ~85% of battery-grade graphite with no credible western alternative at scale within 5 years. Full domestic lithium mining would require sacrificing enormous swaths of the American West. The environmental math simply doesn't work for full domestic self-sufficiency on raw materials.

🇦🇺 Lynas (world's largest non-China REE producer) · 🇯🇵 Japan processing R&D · 🇧🇷 Serra Verde (developing)
Manpower Critical
~5,000 researchers nationally; ~280 PhDs/yr. When Mountain Pass closed in 2002, this community of practice dispersed and retired. Cannot be recovered from textbooks.
Education gap Critical
Hydrometallurgy and rare earth separation chemistry are nearly extinct US specialties. China has 20+ years of compounding institutional knowledge lead that cannot be shortcut.
Environmental impact Severe
REE processing generates radioactive waste (thorium, uranium co-extraction), acid mine drainage, and heavy metal contamination. Mountain Pass closed partly due to pipeline spills. Among the most toxic industrial processes in existence.
Why partial — and why heavy REE processing should be Australia's problem, not ours
The US should reshore light REE magnet production (Neodymium — MP Materials is already building this). Heavy REE processing (Dysprosium, Terbium for F-35 and EV motors) should be processed in Australia under strict environmental standards, not in the American West. The radioactive waste problem is real and long-lasting. The US stopped doing this processing partly because of environmental consequences. Reconstituting it at full scale domestically would require either accepting generational environmental damage or exempting defense processing from environmental law — neither is acceptable.

Environmental impact Very High

The self-defeating tariff problem
The US 50% tariff on EU and Canadian steel/aluminum is directly taxing our best allied steel sources while trying to build domestic capacity. This is the clearest example of policy working against itself. Electrical steel for grid transformers (critical for energy transition) has a genuine domestic security case. Commodity carbon steel does not. Maintain a modern domestic electric arc furnace industry for specialty grades; let Canada and Germany supply the rest.

Better model: Germany and Japan supply the technology and cells; Mexico and US provide final assembly and installation; US focuses on inverters, grid integration, and software. Don't try to out-China China at wafer manufacturing — it's economically irrational.

🇦🇺 Lithium/cobalt/nickel · 🇨🇱🇵🇪 Copper · 🇦🇷 Lithium · 🇨🇦 Nickel/cobalt · 🇧🇷 REEs (developing)
Manpower Critical
Fewer than 400 mining engineers graduated per year nationally. Programs have been closing for decades. A 10–15 year pipeline rebuild even if started immediately today.
Permitting timeline Catastrophic
Average mine permitting in the US takes 29 years. This single number makes full domestic mining effectively impossible on any strategic timeline. Reform required first.
Environmental impact Catastrophic
Acid mine drainage lasts centuries. Heavy metal leaching into water tables supplying agriculture and drinking water. Lithium mining in Nevada and the West destroys arid ecosystems and water supplies. This is literally the "living in a mining pit" scenario.
The environmental case against full domestic mining is overwhelming
The US has the minerals. It does not have the political, environmental, or permitting infrastructure to extract them at the scale required — and attempting to do so would sacrifice public lands, aquifers, and community health for generations. The right answer is strategic stockpiling + allied mining (Australia, Chile, Argentina, Canada, Brazil) + domestic refining of imported ores. You get the security without turning the American West into a sacrifice zone. The $12B strategic stockpile initiative announced in 2026 is the correct first move. Pair it with allied mining agreements — not a domestic mining boom that the permitting system cannot deliver anyway.

Manpower Catastrophic
Industry is essentially gone. Only 3 accredited naval architecture programs remain in the US. The expertise has literally retired out of existence in several specialty areas over the past 40 years.
Education gap Critical
Naval architecture rebuilding would take 15–20 years minimum. Shipbuilding is a craft as much as a science — institutional knowledge lost when shipyards closed cannot be recovered from programs alone.
Environmental impact High
Heavy industry, toxic marine coatings, blast and paint operations, coastal real estate impact, dry dock construction. Not catastrophic but significant and requires premium coastal locations already under development pressure.
Why this is the clearest case for leaving offshore — with a specific exception
South Korea's yards are the world's finest. Japan's are excellent. The institutional knowledge, workforce, and physical infrastructure to rebuild commercial US shipbuilding simply no longer exists, and reconstructing it would take 30+ years and trillions of dollars while producing ships that cost 3–5x more than allied equivalents. The right answer: maintain a minimal domestic yard for classified naval programs (submarines, classified surface ships where security matters); buy everything else from Korea and Japan. Reform the Jones Act for domestic routes to allow allied-built vessels.

🇹🇼 Design+fab · 🇰🇷 Components · 🇲🇽 Final assembly · 🇯🇵 Precision machinery · 🇩🇪 Industrial equipment
Manpower Astronomical
Would require recreating the Chinese manufacturing ecosystem — hundreds of thousands of assembly workers plus the entire supplier network built over 30 years. US wages alone make this uneconomic.
Cost to consumer Extreme
Full domestic smartphone manufacturing would cost 2–4x current prices. A $1,000 phone becomes $3,000–4,000. This is a direct tax on ordinary Americans with zero security benefit — allied manufacturing serves the same purpose at a fraction of the cost.
Environmental impact Moderate
Chemical etching, rare metal use, e-waste. Not catastrophic but would concentrate industrial pollution in US manufacturing corridors that currently don't have it.
The clearest case for comparative advantage — US competitive position is in design and IP, not assembly
Taiwan designs the most advanced chips. Korea makes memory and displays. Japan makes precision components and machinery. Mexico assembles competitively near the US border. The US wins in this ecosystem by owning the design IP, the software ecosystem, and the high-value component technology — not by competing with Asian factories on assembly labor. Full reshore of consumer electronics would impoverish ordinary Americans, generate enormous industrial pollution, require a workforce that doesn't exist, and produce no national security benefit that allied manufacturing doesn't already provide. This is the poster child for "comparative advantage is not a bug, it's the point."

The Honest Policy Conclusion

Of 10 critical reshoring needs, only 2 are unambiguous "must reshore" cases on national security grounds: defense electronics and semiconductors. Both require maintained immigration and allied partnerships to achieve their timelines — neither is achievable through domestic-only policy alone.

Four industries warrant partial reshoring — pharmaceuticals, batteries, rare earths, and specialty steel — where the answer is "reshore the strategic piece, ally-source the rest." In every case, the constraint that limits full domestic production is a combination of workforce gaps, environmental damage, and economic cost that no tariff can resolve.

Three industries should be left with allied manufacturers as a matter of economic and environmental rationality: commercial shipbuilding, consumer electronics, and large-scale solar manufacturing. The US competitive position in all three is in IP, design, and software — not in factories. Trying to compete on assembly is a direct tax on American consumers with no offsetting security benefit.

The environmental dimension is systematically underweighted in reshoring debates. Full domestic critical mineral mining would sacrifice aquifers and public lands for generations. Full rare earth processing would generate radioactive waste in American communities. Full steel and solar manufacturing would concentrate heavy industrial pollution in new communities. The global distribution of dirty industries was not purely exploitation — it was also a distribution of environmental burden that made American quality of life possible.

The timeline that actually works: allied cooperation compresses a 30–40 year solo reshoring timeline to 10–20 years for the industries that genuinely need it. The only thing that makes it fail is abandoning the alliances before the domestic capacity is built — which is precisely the risk the current policy trajectory creates.
