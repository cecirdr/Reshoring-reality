// Bottom line — R&D field assessment
Of the ten critical reshoring areas, three have catastrophic researcher gaps (rare earths, mining, shipbuilding) where the knowledge base has literally retired out of existence, six have thin but recoverable pipelines that would require 10–15 years of sustained investment to scale, and one — pharmaceuticals — has a moderate foundation that could be built upon within a decade if process manufacturing expertise receives targeted investment. None are ready now. None could be made ready within a single presidential term.

The visa trap
The US built its frontier R&D workforce substantially on foreign talent — graduate students, postdocs, H-1B holders. If immigration policy tightens at the same moment decoupling creates demand for more domestic talent, the pipeline does not automatically fill with native-born Americans. It collapses. The domestic replacement pipeline runs 8–15 years from K–12 investment to working researcher. You cannot vote to replace it on a two-year election cycle. Countries that are restricting immigration while simultaneously trying to reshore are creating a scissors crisis — cutting supply precisely when demand is rising. China, South Korea, and Germany are all actively recruiting the researchers the US is making unwelcome.
The salary competition problem
The best STEM PhDs in the US go to software, AI, and data science because compensation is 2–3x higher than industrial manufacturing R&D. A semiconductor process engineer earns $140–180K. A comparably skilled machine learning engineer at a major tech firm earns $250–400K. Reshoring requires that gap to close significantly — or the talent will not move into chips, mining, or shipbuilding regardless of how many programs are funded. You can subsidize the factory. You cannot easily subsidize away a $200K salary gap. Until industrial R&D compensation becomes competitive, training more people is partially futile: you train them for manufacturing and they go work in tech.
Institutional knowledge cannot be recreated quickly
Rare earth processing, shipbuilding, and specialty metallurgy are crafts as much as sciences. When Mountain Pass closed in 2002, the community of practice that knew how to run it dispersed and largely retired. That knowledge lives in the heads of people who are no longer working, not in textbooks or patents. China has been compounding that institutional advantage for 20+ years — training cohort after cohort of specialists, building tacit knowledge that accumulates at the bench level, not in academic papers. No amount of investment can shortcut the time required to rebuild a living community of practice. The honest answer for rare earths, specialty metallurgy, and shipbuilding is that meaningful domestic capacity is a 25–35 year project under the most favorable conditions imaginable.
The sequencing problem — disruption before capacity
The single most dangerous element of the current policy trajectory is sequence. Successful industrial transition requires building domestic capacity first, then transitioning off foreign supply chains — not the reverse. What is happening now is the opposite: supply chains are being disrupted through tariffs and decoupling before domestic replacements exist. This creates a window of maximal vulnerability where the US has neither the old supply chain nor the new one. That window could last a decade or more. During that window, adversaries who control the disrupted supply chains have direct leverage over US industrial output, defense readiness, and consumer welfare — which is precisely what 2025's rare earth and legacy chip export restrictions demonstrated in real time.
// Unequivocal bottom line
The US has world-class knowledge institutions but a dangerously thin industrial application workforce in exactly the fields reshoring demands most. The talent shortage is real, structural, and multi-decade in depth. It cannot be resolved by tariffs, by executive order, or within any single administration's timeline. The only realistic path runs through sustained K–12 STEM investment, open immigration for critical talent, allied supply chain cooperation as a bridge to domestic capacity, and patient decades-long industrial policy insulated from electoral cycles. Attempting full domestic self-sufficiency is not a strategic option — it is a fantasy that would require environmental sacrifice, population that doesn't exist, and timelines that span generations. The evidence points overwhelmingly toward managed interdependence with strategic redundancy, not autarky.
